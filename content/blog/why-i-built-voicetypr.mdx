The email arrived on a Tuesday. *Your subscription renews on the 17th. No action needed.*

I had been dictating into Cursor and Claude for about six hours that day. My wrists were grateful. My credit card was less so. The annual receipt from my cloud dictation app would clear in nine days for another $180. That was about the same as the cost of a decent mechanical keyboard, which I would also probably buy this year, because that&rsquo;s what indie hacking does to your gear budget.

I closed the email and opened a fresh terminal. Two hours later I had a Rust crate that called Whisper.cpp, a hotkey listener, and a paper napkin sketch of a tray icon. Eight months later that crate became VoiceTypr.

This is the story of why.

## The thing nobody puts in the pitch deck

I dictate for a living. Not in the &ldquo;executive on a phone call&rdquo; sense. In the &ldquo;solo founder typing prompts at AI for ten hours a day&rdquo; sense.

The shape of my work changed quietly some time around when Cursor got good. Most of the day I am writing prose at a model: bug reports, PR descriptions, Slack replies, specs nobody asked for, prompts long enough that I copy them into a notes app first. I&rsquo;m not really *coding* anymore. I&rsquo;m typing English at a compiler that happens to output TypeScript.

Typing English at the speed I think is exhausting. My brain runs at maybe 400 words per minute. My fingers, on a good day, manage 80. The gap between those two numbers is the gap between &ldquo;fun&rdquo; and &ldquo;the part of the day I avoid.&rdquo;

So I started dictating. Cloud-based, of course. Subscription-based, of course. It worked.

It worked the way a thing works when you&rsquo;re paying $15/month and trying not to look at the bill.

## What I actually wanted to stop doing

Three things were grinding me down. They&rsquo;re obvious in retrospect, easy to miss while you&rsquo;re inside the experience.

**My audio was leaving my machine.** Every &ldquo;hold this button to dictate&rdquo; sent a stream of my voice to a server I had never met. The privacy policy said the right things. The product worked. But sometimes I was dictating early notes about features that didn&rsquo;t exist yet, into a service whose business model needed my data to be useful in some statistical sense. I would not say I was paranoid. I would say I noticed.

**The internet was a dependency for typing.** I do my best work in airports and on planes. Cloud dictation does not work in airports and planes. I could have switched to Apple&rsquo;s built-in dictation, except Apple&rsquo;s built-in dictation hallucinates roughly one out of every seven proper nouns and gives up after about thirty seconds. I tried. I lost work. I went back to the cloud. I paid the bill.

**The subscription was math I didn&rsquo;t love.** A dictation app is not a video game with new content shipping monthly. It transcribes audio. The model gets better, sure. The platform support gets wider, sure. But the core loop is: I press a button, words appear, I keep working. Why am I paying for that loop forever?

I sat with that question for a few weeks. I didn&rsquo;t want to *cancel* and lose dictation. I wanted to *own* dictation. The way you own a copy of Sublime Text or BBEdit or Things 3. Pay once. Use it for ten years. Update it because you want the new version, not because the old one stops working.

There was no offline, lifetime-priced version of what I was using. There was no pay-once equivalent of [Wispr Flow](/wispr-flow-alternative). There were a few open-source projects that ran Whisper locally, but each one was either a CLI for engineers, a half-built menu bar app, or a wrapper that pasted into one specific text field on macOS only. I wanted the *whole product*. Hotkey-driven, paste-into-anything, Mac and Windows, no account, no cloud.

So I shipped one.

## The weekend that became eight months

I gave myself a weekend. The weekend was a lie I told myself; it always is.

The first Saturday I had Whisper.cpp transcribing audio from a microphone in Rust. The first Sunday I had a Tauri app that could record, transcribe, and paste the result wherever the cursor was. By Monday I had a working prototype on my own machine that did 80% of what my paid subscription did. I told my partner I had &ldquo;just figured something out&rdquo; and would &ldquo;wrap it up next weekend.&rdquo;

The next eight months were the other 20%.

A short, incomplete list of things I learned were harder than they sounded:

- Reliable global hotkeys on macOS that don&rsquo;t conflict with the OS, screen readers, accessibility tooling, or three other apps that all claim &lt;kbd&gt;Cmd+Shift+Space&lt;/kbd&gt;.
- Pasting into the active app *correctly*, including apps that strip formatting, apps that need clipboard history preserved, apps that fight you (looking at you, certain Electron text editors).
- Selecting the right Whisper model size by default for hardware nobody told you about. Tiny on a laptop trying to render a Figma file is unusable. Large on an M1 Air is unusable in a different direction.
- Apple Silicon vs Intel: same OS, different inference paths, different memory footprints, different optimization strategies. I ship a separate Intel build for a reason.
- Windows: GPU detection across NVIDIA, AMD, and Intel. Driver-version sensitivity. The way Windows decides today is the day to install a 2GB update mid-recording.
- Code signing on Windows when you don&rsquo;t have $300/year for an EV cert lying around.

If you&rsquo;ve never shipped desktop software, this list looks like a chore. If you have, you&rsquo;re wincing in solidarity. Cross-platform native apps are a tax you pay forever. The cloud dictation services I left behind don&rsquo;t pay this tax. They run on a Linux server somewhere. They get one machine to work and bill you forever for the privilege of streaming audio to it.

I am paying the tax so my customers don&rsquo;t have to.

## Why local. Why Tauri. Why pay once.

Three decisions defined what VoiceTypr would become. None of them were obvious at the time.

**Local-first, audio-stays-here.** I could have built a thin wrapper over a cloud API and shipped a month sooner. I would have lost the only feature I actually cared about: nobody else hears me. Whisper running locally is &mdash; on modern hardware &mdash; genuinely faster than streaming audio to a cloud in most contexts, because you&rsquo;re not paying the round-trip latency. It&rsquo;s also genuinely private, in the boring legal sense and the &ldquo;I can dictate ideas about competitors without feeling weird&rdquo; sense.

**Tauri instead of Electron.** I like Electron. I have shipped Electron apps. For VoiceTypr I needed memory headroom for the model, which meant the chrome had to stay tiny. Tauri uses the OS&rsquo;s native webview and a Rust backend; the resulting app is around 30&ndash;40 MB on disk and idles at a few hundred MB of RAM, most of which is the Whisper weights. The same app in Electron would be a 200 MB Chromium tax that competes with the model for the user&rsquo;s actual work.

**Pay once, lifetime updates.** This is the contrarian one. Every founder Twitter thread I read said *recurring revenue, recurring revenue, recurring revenue.* I get it. I would also rather have someone pay once and have an opt-in upgrade conversation later than a subscription receipt that turns my product into background guilt for the buyer. People who pay once and use a tool for five years do not write angry tweets about pricing. They tell their friends. Subscription customers, particularly for a tool they use *constantly*, eventually start to notice the subscription. Then they start to notice it more. Then they cancel.

If you want the math: VoiceTypr starts at $39 once for one device, with higher device counts for people who use multiple machines. I assume an average lifetime usage of three years. The annualized revenue is small, but the customer relationship is honest. They didn&rsquo;t buy a subscription. They bought a tool. The next time they upgrade their machine they don&rsquo;t check whether their dictation app still works. They just install it. That&rsquo;s the product I wanted to be on the buying side of, so it&rsquo;s the product I built.

## The parts that humbled me

I don&rsquo;t want to make the building sound clean. It wasn&rsquo;t.

The first three weeks of beta, my support inbox was an obituary list of edge cases I had not anticipated. Bluetooth microphones that worked everywhere except VoiceTypr. International keyboards that broke the hotkey detection. Users on macOS Ventura who didn&rsquo;t know they needed to grant accessibility permissions. A particularly memorable bug where pasting into a Slack message thread on Windows would briefly insert the text twice, which I traced to a focus race condition that took two days to reproduce in isolation.

I rewrote the audio capture layer twice. The model selection UI, three times. The activation flow, four times.

There are still things I get wrong. There are users on Intel Macs running 16 GB of RAM who cannot run the medium Whisper model comfortably and don&rsquo;t know that, so they pick it, and it&rsquo;s slow, and they email me. I should detect their hardware and recommend small. I will. Not this week. There&rsquo;s a list.

Ship date is a single point in time. Quality is a function over months. I am still iterating on the function.

## Where it is now

VoiceTypr ships on macOS 13+ (Apple Silicon and Intel) and Windows 10+. It runs locally. It supports 99+ languages. The license is one-time. The trial is three days, no card. The source for the core is on [GitHub](https://github.com/moinulmoin/voicetypr), because the only reason cloud dictation companies don&rsquo;t open source theirs is that the cloud bit *is* the moat, and I don&rsquo;t have that problem.

I&rsquo;m currently working on:

- Better automatic model recommendations based on detected hardware.
- Faster startup on cold launch, especially on Windows.
- Sharper setup flows for people moving between multiple machines.

I&rsquo;m not winning. I&rsquo;m not pretending to. I&rsquo;m a solo founder shipping a desktop app against companies with seed rounds. Some weeks I write code. Some weeks I write support replies. Some weeks I write blog posts like this one, in dictation, because the irony is too good to pass up.

If any of this resonates &mdash; if you also dictate at AI for a living and the renewal emails are starting to pile up &mdash; the [trial](/download) is three days, no card. Try it on a flight. That&rsquo;s actually a good test.

The audio stays on your machine. The license stays in your inbox. And next year, when April rolls around, your dictation app will not be sending you a renewal email.

That&rsquo;s the version of this product I wanted as a customer, so that&rsquo;s the version I shipped.
