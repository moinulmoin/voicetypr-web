Three weeks into using VoiceTypr full-time inside [Cursor](https://cursor.com), I caught myself typing a prompt and felt physically odd about it. Like reaching for a phone that wasn&rsquo;t in my pocket. The voice loop had become the default and the keyboard had become the fallback.

This is what the workflow looks like after thirty days. The parts that worked, the parts that didn&rsquo;t, and the small adjustments that made it feel native rather than gimmicky.

## The setup

I am writing this on macOS with an M2 Air. The setup is identical on Windows; the hotkey is the only thing that changes.

- Hotkey: <kbd>&#8984;&#8679;Space</kbd> on macOS, <kbd>Ctrl+Shift+Space</kbd> on Windows
- Mode: hold-to-talk (release to transcribe). Push-to-talk (toggle) exists but I keep tripping it
- Model: medium Whisper. Tiny is too lossy on dense technical vocabulary; large is overkill for the kind of prompts I write
- AI preset: &ldquo;Prompts.&rdquo; This is one of the four built-in formatting profiles &mdash; it cleans filler, normalizes punctuation, and otherwise doesn&rsquo;t touch your wording

Total wall-clock time from &ldquo;hold&rdquo; to &ldquo;text in cursor&rsquo;s composer&rdquo; on this hardware: under a second for short prompts, two seconds for a long one.

## The five things I dictate every day

### 1. Prompts to the agent

This is 60% of the daily voice usage. Cursor&rsquo;s agent prompt box is where I previously typed the longest blocks of English in my workflow &mdash; specs, requirements, &ldquo;here&rsquo;s what&rsquo;s happening, here&rsquo;s what I want.&rdquo;

What changed: I now talk through the problem the way I would explain it to a colleague, then hold the hotkey and say all of it. The Prompts preset cleans up the &ldquo;ums&rdquo; and stitches sentences. The agent gets a noticeably better prompt than I would have typed, because *typing prompts makes me tighten them*. Tighter is not always better. Sometimes the agent needs the meandering middle.

### 2. Commit messages

I never wrote good commit messages when I had to type them. They were always &ldquo;wip&rdquo; or &ldquo;fix.&rdquo; Voice changed that overnight. I hold the hotkey, say the change in two sentences, hit enter. The result is a commit message that explains why, not just what.

The pattern that works: present-tense imperative for the subject (&ldquo;refresh checkout flow to use V1 routes&rdquo;), one paragraph of context for the body. I dictate both into the same VoiceTypr capture.

### 3. PR descriptions

This is the unexpected win.

Most of my PRs used to ship with a one-liner because writing the full description felt like writing a homework assignment. I would close the dialog, write &ldquo;see commits,&rdquo; merge.

With voice, a five-paragraph PR description costs about ninety seconds of speaking. Reviewers actually have context. I have caught at least three bugs in the last month by virtue of saying my own change out loud and noticing the gap between what I said and what the diff did.

If you take exactly one thing from this post: dictate your PR descriptions for a week and watch what changes about your reviews.

### 4. Code comments and JSDoc

This is the most divisive one. Code comments are a contested artefact &mdash; some teams want them, some don&rsquo;t. In code that I&rsquo;ll re-read in six months, I now leave a sentence or two of *why*, not what, dictated in the moment.

The trick is dictating *during* the implementation, not after. By the time you&rsquo;re done with a function the rationale has already faded. Dictate the why mid-write, while the constraint is still loud in your head.

### 5. Slack and Linear

I batch Slack at the end of work blocks. Voice makes the batching cheap. Three updates, two replies, one Linear comment, ninety seconds. I used to drag this out across the day. Now it&rsquo;s a punctuation mark between focus blocks.

## Parts of the workflow that didn&rsquo;t survive

Honest list. Everything that *didn&rsquo;t* stick is also data.

**Dictating actual code.** Tried it for a week. I would say &ldquo;const foo equals null&rdquo; and the model would render &ldquo;const four equals nul&rdquo; or some variation. Code-mode dictation is a separate problem from prose-mode dictation. I went back to typing code; voice is for English.

**Dictating tests.** Same problem in a different costume. Test names become prose and pass through fine; assertions become regex character soup.

**Dictating in noisy spaces.** A coffee shop with a single hum is fine. A coffee shop with people talking nearby is not. The transcription degrades roughly linearly with background noise. AirPods Pro 2 with active noise cancelling helps; the built-in MacBook mic in a busy room does not.

**Dictating long uninterrupted blocks.** Past ninety seconds of continuous speaking the model starts to lose context on the early parts. I now break long thoughts into two or three captures with explicit pauses.

## What I would change about the workflow

If I were starting over with what I know now:

- Set a global rule that prompts go through the &ldquo;Prompts&rdquo; preset and Slack/messaging goes through &ldquo;Default.&rdquo; The presets behave differently and matter more than I initially thought.
- Use the Esc-Esc cancel gesture earlier in the learning curve. For the first week I would dictate badly, paste, then delete and retry. Esc-Esc abandons a recording before it&rsquo;s pasted. This is the second-most-used keystroke in my workflow now after the hotkey itself.
- Start with the medium model on day one, not tiny. The accuracy delta is large enough that beginners give up if they start on tiny and hear &ldquo;Cursor&rdquo; come out as &ldquo;Carter.&rdquo;

## The thing nobody tells you about voice typing

The interesting effect &mdash; the one that took me by surprise &mdash; is that voice changes *what you write*, not just *how fast you write it*. Typed prompts are economical because typing is expensive. Voice prompts are exploratory because voice is cheap. You end up writing prompts you wouldn&rsquo;t have bothered to type, and some of them turn out to be the right prompts.

The same is true of comments, PR descriptions, and Slack updates. Voice doesn&rsquo;t just make existing artifacts faster to produce. It changes which artifacts exist at all.

I would not have predicted that on day one. It is the actual reason I no longer reach for the keyboard.
